While lots of work in aerospace medicine is performed by medical professionals and flight surgeons, there is also a large community of researchers who investigate a variety of areas related to humans in aerospace environments. These scientists make use of historical data from flight and space experiments, extreme pressure environments and analogs, as well as a variety of experimental and analytical tools to understand and improve the health, safety, and performance of divers, pilots, and astronauts. Hot research areas and recent relevant articles:


1) Cardiovascular system function in aerospace environments

“Assessment of Jugular Venous Blood Flow Stasis and Thrombosis During Spaceflight”
⇛ The mechanism for blood leaving the head is important for preventing blood clots (thrombus) that can cause stroke. The goal of this experiment was to study blood flow in 11 astronauts living in microgravity on the International Space Station (ISS). The results have big implications for future missions and possibly for crew selection.

2) Social isolation and team dynamics in long-duration missions

“COVID-19—The largest isolation study in history: the value of shared learn- ings from spaceflight analogs”
⇛ Lockdowns and quarantines due to the COVID-19 pandemic have created environ- ments for the public that are isolated, confined, and extreme (ICE), with stressors similar to those experienced by astronauts during space exploration missions.

3) Etiology of SANS and potential countermeasures

“Spaceflight associated neuro-ocular syndrome (SANS) and the neuro-ophthalmologic effects of microgravity: a review and an update”
⇛ Space Flight-Associated Neuro-ocular Syndrome (SANS), is still not well understood and remains a risk and knowledge gap for NASA’s Human Research Program. This article reviews the current body of research into SANS and its causes.
