Speaker Highlight - Shelby Hopkins

Shelby Hopkins is a senior systems engineer at Lockheed Martin Space. She currently works on Human Landing System (HLS), a NASA program aiming to bring the first woman and the next man to the surface of the Moon by 2024. On the HLS program, she is the integrator for the Environmental Control and Life Support Systems (ECLSS) for the National Team (Blue Origin, Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman). Shelby previously has been an ECLSS analyst for the Orion Spacecraft and the NextSTEP Habitat. Shelby graduated from the Georgia Institute of Technology with a Bachelor of Science in Aerospace Engineering. She also holds a Master of Science in Aerospace Engineering from the University of Colorado Boulder, where she also was a Graduate Research Assistant at BioServe Space Technologies.

Shelby will be presenting to the CU Aerospace Medicine Resident Organization (CU AMSRO) Chapter on Monday, March 8th at 5:00pm on ECLSS design for human spaceflight

Aerospace Medicine and ECLSS Designs

An Environmental Control and Life Support System (ECLSS) is a critical system to keep the crew alive in a spacecraft. It provides functionalities such as CO2 removal, O2 provision, water management, waste management, temperature & humidity control, and trace contaminant removal to satisfy the crew’s metabolic needs. Aerospace medicine plays an essential role in designing ECLSS. For instance, let’s think about determining the optimal CO2 level in a spacecraft. In doing so, the aerospace medicine field physicians would ask, “What CO2 level is safe for the crew?”. In contrast, the ECLSS engineers would ask, “How would the CO2 level requirement affect the CO2 removal system design?”. While physicians desire a lower CO2 level possible, it would become much more difficult for engineers to develop hardware with such high standards. To design a reliable ECLSS, physicians and engineers would often have to work hand in hand and find a “good enough” spot that satisfies both sides’ needs.

To learn more about ECLSS functionalities and requirements, check the documents below:

  • NASA Life Support Baseline Values and Assumptions Document
  • NASA Exposure Guidelines (SMACs & SWEGs)
  • Wieland, P. O. "Living Together in Space: The Design and Operation of the Life Support Systems on the International Space Station. Volume 1." (1998).

What is CU AMSRO?

  • Who are we?:
